Audio samples of "A Semi-supervised Method to Implement End-to-end Speech Synthesis for Tone Sandhi Language"

Authors: Pengfei Shao, Minchuan Chen, Jun Ma, Shaojun Wang, Jing Xiao

End-to-end generative text-to-speech model, such as Tacotron, synthesizes speech directly from raw text. This typically requires a sizeable set of high-quality pairs for training. Recent works show that it outperforms classical pipeline systems with regards to naturalness in English, Chinese Mandarin and some other languages. As one of the major Chinese dialects, Wu dialect is well known for its complex tone sandhi system. Therefore it could be much more difficult to achieve an end-to-end speech synthesis system, largely due to its variable tone forms. Moreover it is hard to label the tone descriptions on large scale audio database precisely. Taking Shanghainese, a Wu dialect, as an example, we propose a method that can construct the tone sandhi rules more efficiently only by labeling a small amount of data. The speech synthesis system based on the proposed rules shows better results on both objective and subjective evaluation compared to the system using traditional tone sandhi rules or using citation tone only. These generalized rules can also be used for the front-end stage of speech synthesis as well.

Subjective evaluations

The following three speech synthesis systems are established for subjective preference tests in this paper.

  • Baseline system: None tone sandhi was used. We give each syllable their citation tone for training. So there are only 5 citation tone categories, 53, 34, 23, 55, 12.
  • Tra-SH system: Traditional tone sandhi rules shown in Table 1 of this paper are used. There are 10 tone categories, 53, 34, 23, 55, 12, 21, 33, 44, 11, 22.
  • Pro-SH system: Tone sandhi rules shown in Table 2 of this paper are used in our proposed system. There are 13 tone categories, 53, 34, 23, 50, 12, 55, 42, 21, 33, 44, 21, 11, 22.
